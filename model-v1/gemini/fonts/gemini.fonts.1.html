Topic: The Chain Rule in Calculus

Welcome back. In this lesson, we are going to learn how to differentiate composite functions. Imagine you have a function nested inside another function, like a set of Russian dolls. If we let \( h(x) = f(g(x)) \), we cannot just take the derivative of the outside and ignore the inside. We have to apply the Chain Rule.

The formal definition of the chain rule states that if \( g \) is differentiable at \( x \) and \( f \) is differentiable at \( g(x) \), then the composite function \( h \) is differentiable at \( x \), and its derivative is given by: $$ h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) $$

Let's try a concrete example to see this in action. Suppose we want to find the derivative of \( y = (3x^2 + 1)^5 \). Here, our inner function is \( u = 3x^2 + 1 \) and our outer function is \( y = u^5 \). $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \\ &= \frac{d}{du}(u^5) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(3x^2 + 1) \\ &= 5u^4 \cdot (6x) \\ &= 5(3x^2 + 1)^4 \cdot 6x \\ &= 30x(3x^2 + 1)^4 \end{aligned} $$

Notice how we "peeled the onion"? We took the derivative of the outside power function first, kept the inside exactly the same, and then multiplied by the derivative of the inside. This pattern appears everywhere in calculus, especially when dealing with trigonometric functions like \( \sin(x^2) \) or exponential growth models.

Concept: Bayes' Theorem

Our intuition about probability is often wrong. We tend to confuse the probability of a cause given an effect with the probability of an effect given a cause. Bayes' Theorem provides the mathematical framework to update our beliefs when we receive new evidence.

Mathematically, for two events \( A \) and \( B \), the theorem is stated as: $$ P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} $$

The Medical Test Paradox
Imagine a disease that affects 1% of the population (\( P(D) = 0.01 \)). You take a test that is 99% accurate. This means if you have the disease, it tests positive 99% of the time (\( P(+|D) = 0.99 \)), and if you don't, it tests negative 99% of the time. You test positive. What is the chance you actually have the disease?

Most people guess 99%. Let's calculate the true probability \( P(D|+) \) using the total probability rule for the denominator: $$ \begin{aligned} P(D|+) &= \frac{P(+|D)P(D)}{P(+|D)P(D) + P(+|D^c)P(D^c)} \\ &= \frac{(0.99)(0.01)}{(0.99)(0.01) + (0.01)(0.99)} \\ &= \frac{0.0099}{0.0099 + 0.0099} \\ &= \frac{0.0099}{0.0198} = 0.5 \end{aligned} $$

Surprisingly, the answer is only 50%. Because the disease is so rare, the number of "false positives" from the healthy population matches the number of "true positives" from the sick population. Navier–Stokes equations

The Navier–Stokes equations are a set of partial differential equations which describe the motion of viscous fluid substances. Use of the Navier–Stokes equations usually begins with the definition of the fluid properties (such as density, viscosity, and thermal conductivity) and the boundary conditions of the domain of interest.

In an inertial frame of reference, the general form of the equations of fluid motion is: $$ \rho \left( \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{u} \right) = -\nabla p + \nabla \cdot \left[ \mu (\nabla \mathbf{u} + (\nabla \mathbf{u})^T) \right] + \mathbf{f} $$

Where:

  • \( \mathbf{u} \) is the fluid velocity vector field
  • \( p \) is the fluid pressure field
  • \( \rho \) is the fluid density
  • \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity

For the specific case of an incompressible Newtonian fluid (where density \( \rho \) is constant and divergence is zero), the equations simplify significantly. The continuity equation becomes \( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0 \), and the momentum equation reduces to: $$ \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} - \nu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \mathbf{g} $$

Despite their wide use in modeling weather, ocean currents, and air flow over wings, rigorous mathematical proof of the existence and smoothness of solutions in three dimensions remains one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems.

Compute the definite integral of the function \( x^2 e^{-x} \) from zero to infinity. $$ \int_{0}^{\infty} x^2 e^{-x} \, dx $$

Step-by-step solution:
We can solve this using integration by parts, where \( \int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du \). Let \( u = x^2 \) and \( dv = e^{-x}dx \).

First iteration: $$ \begin{aligned} \int x^2 e^{-x} \, dx &= -x^2 e^{-x} - \int -2x e^{-x} \, dx \\ &= -x^2 e^{-x} + 2 \int x e^{-x} \, dx \end{aligned} $$ Now we perform integration by parts again on the remaining integral \( \int x e^{-x} \, dx \): $$ \begin{aligned} \int x e^{-x} \, dx &= -x e^{-x} - \int -e^{-x} \, dx \\ &= -x e^{-x} - e^{-x} \end{aligned} $$

Result:
Combining these terms and evaluating the limits from \( 0 \) to \( \infty \): $$ \left[ -e^{-x}(x^2 + 2x + 2) \right]_0^{\infty} = 0 - (-2) = 2 $$ The integral converges to 2.

Formatting Mathematical Proofs

When writing formal proofs in LaTeX, it is essential to align your logical connectives properly. We often use the `align` environment for multi-step deductions. Below is an example of a formal proof regarding limit definitions, often used in Real Analysis courses.

Theorem: The limit of sum is the sum of limits.
Proof: Let \( \epsilon > 0 \) be given. We know that \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L \) and \( \lim_{x \to c} g(x) = M \). This implies there exist \( \delta_1, \delta_2 > 0 \) such that: $$ 0 < |x - c| < \delta_1 \implies |f(x) - L| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} $$ $$ 0 < |x - c| < \delta_2 \implies |g(x) - M| < \frac{\epsilon}{2} $$

Choose \( \delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2) \). Then for all \( x \) satisfying \( 0 < |x - c| < \delta \), we have: $$ \begin{aligned} |(f(x) + g(x)) - (L + M)| &=|(f(x) - L) + (g(x) - M)| \\ &\leq |f(x) - L| + |g(x) - M| \quad (\text{Triangle Inequality}) \\ &< \frac{\epsilon}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} \\ &=\epsilon \end{aligned} $$

Thus, by definition, \( \lim_{x \to c} [f(x) + g(x)] = L + M \). Q.E.D.

Abstract: Solutions to the Schrödinger Equation

We investigate the behavior of a particle in a one-dimensional infinite potential well. The time-independent Schrödinger equation is given by \( \hat{H}\psi = E\psi \), where the Hamiltonian operator includes both kinetic and potential energy terms.

Inside the well (\( 0 < x < L \)), the potential \( V(x)=0 \), reducing the equation to: $$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}=E\psi \quad \implies \quad \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + k^2\psi=0 $$ where we define the wave number \( k=\frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar} \). The general solution to this differential equation is of the form: $$ \psi(x)=A \sin(kx) + B \cos(kx) $$

Applying boundary conditions \( \psi(0) = 0 \) and \( \psi(L) = 0 \), we find that \( B=0 \) and \( kL = n\pi \) for integer \( n \). This leads to the quantization of energy levels: $$ E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots $$ The normalized wave functions are thus derived as \( \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \). These results are consistent with standard quantum mechanical predictions.

Machine Learning: Gradient Descent

In this module, we discuss how to minimize the cost function \( J(\theta) \) for linear regression. The goal is to find the parameters \( \theta_0, \theta_1 \) that minimize the squared error between our hypothesis \( h_\theta(x) \) and the actual data \( y \).

The Mean Squared Error (MSE) cost function is defined as: $$ J(\theta_0, \theta_1) = \frac{1}{2m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left( h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)} \right)^2 $$ where \( m \) is the number of training examples. To find the minimum, we use the Gradient Descent algorithm, which iteratively updates the parameters.

The update rule for a specific parameter \( \theta_j \) is: $$ \theta_j := \theta_j - \alpha \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta_j} J(\theta) $$ Solving for the partial derivative, we get the specific update rule for linear regression: $$ \theta_j := \theta_j - \alpha \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left( h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)} \right) x_j^{(i)} $$

Remember that \( \alpha \) is the learning rate. If \( \alpha \) is too small, convergence is slow. If \( \alpha \) is too large, the function may overshoot the minimum and fail to converge.

Thermodynamics: The Ideal Gas Law

In this chapter, we explore the relationship between the macroscopic properties of gases. An ideal gas is a theoretical gas composed of many randomly moving point particles that are not subject to interparticle interactions.

The state of an ideal gas is determined by the equation: $$ PV = nRT $$

This equation can be derived from the kinetic theory of gases. If we consider the root-mean-square speed of particles, we find that the pressure \( P \) exerted by the gas is related to the average kinetic energy: $$ P = \frac{Nm \overline{v^2}}{3V} $$ Since the average kinetic energy is proportional to temperature (\( \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_B T \)), substituting this back yields the familiar ideal gas law.

Example Problem:
Calculate the volume occupied by 1.00 mol of an ideal gas at STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure, \( T=273.15 \, \text{K} \), \( P=100 \, \text{kPa} \)). $$ V = \frac{nRT}{P} = \frac{(1.00)(8.314)(273.15)}{10^5} \approx 0.0227 \, \text{m}^3 = 22.7 \, \text{L} $$

Number Theory: Euler's Totient Theorem

We conclude our discussion on modular arithmetic with a generalization of Fermat's Little Theorem. Fermat's theorem applies only when the modulus is prime. However, Euler introduced a function, \( \phi(n) \), which counts the positive integers less than \( n \) that are relatively prime to \( n \).

Theorem: If \( a \) and \( n \) are coprime integers (i.e., \( \gcd(a, n) = 1 \)), then: $$ a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n $$

Proof Sketch: Let \( R = \{ r_1, r_2, \dots, r_{\phi(n)} \} \) be the set of integers less than \( n \) coprime to \( n \). If we multiply each element by \( a \), we get a new set \( S = \{ ar_1, ar_2, \dots, ar_{\phi(n)} \} \).

Since \( \gcd(a, n)=1 \), the elements of \( S \) are just a permutation of the elements of \( R \) modulo \( n \). Therefore, the product of elements in \( S \) is congruent to the product of elements in \( R \): $$ \prod_{i=1}^{\phi(n)} (ar_i) \equiv \prod_{i=1}^{\phi(n)} r_i \pmod n $$ $$ a^{\phi(n)} \left( \prod_{i=1}^{\phi(n)} r_i \right) \equiv \left( \prod_{i=1}^{\phi(n)} r_i \right) \pmod n $$ Since the product term is coprime to \( n \), we can cancel it from both sides, leaving \( a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n \).
